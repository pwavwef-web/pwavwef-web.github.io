Leadership Styles & Principles

4 core styles · Situational leadership · Adapting your approach

Leadership is not a fixed personality trait — it is a set of behaviours you can learn, practise and improve. The most effective ambassadors are situational leaders: they read the context and adapt their style accordingly.

The 4 Core Leadership Styles

① Directive

High task focus, low relationship focus. Best when: tight deadlines, new students who need clear instructions, crisis situations.

Example: "Here's exactly what we need to do before the exam next week."

② Coaching

High task + high relationship. Best when: mentoring a newer ambassador or helping a struggling student build confidence.

Example: "What do you think is holding you back? Let's work through it together."

③ Supporting

Low task, high relationship. Best when: a competent student just needs encouragement and emotional support to keep going.

Example: "You've got this — I'm here if you need anything."

④ Delegating

Low task, low relationship. Best when: working with experienced, motivated students who work best with autonomy.

Example: "You're good at this — own it and let me know how it goes."

Applying Situational Leadership

The key question before every interaction is: "What does this person need from me right now?" A stressed final-year student needs support, not direction. A fresh ambassador needs coaching, not delegation. Mismatching your style to the situation is the #1 leadership mistake.

Reflection Exercise

Think of 3 people you currently interact with in your ambassador role (a newer ambassador you're mentoring, a peer you study with, a student you're trying to recruit). Which leadership style would be most effective for each person? Write your answers down and discuss them at your next ambassador check-in.

Running Effective Study Groups

Agenda setting · Roles · Session structure · Accountability

A study group without structure is just a social gathering. As an ambassador, your job is to turn peer study time into a high-output learning session that students want to attend again.

Before the Session: Setting the Agenda

Send the agenda to all participants 24 hours before. A good agenda includes: the topic, the specific sections to be covered, any pre-reading or questions to attempt beforehand, and the session structure with timings.

Assigning Roles for Every Session

Timekeeper

Keeps the session on track. Calls out when a section has 2 minutes left so the presenter can wrap up.

Note-Taker

Captures key points on a shared Google Doc. Posts the summary in the WhatsApp group within 2 hours of the session ending.

Question-Master

Prepares 3–5 exam-style questions related to the topic. Leads the practice Q&A at the end of the session.

Facilitator (You)

Keeps energy high, manages dynamics, ensures everyone participates, and closes the session with next steps.

Keeping Accountability High

At the end of every session, ask: "What is the one thing you'll do before we meet next time?" Write every person's commitment on the shared doc. At the start of the next session, do a quick check-in on whether they did it. Accountability is what separates effective study groups from casual ones.

Practice Exercise

Create a detailed agenda for your next study group session. Include: topic, pre-reading, 4 agenda items with timings, assigned roles, and the closing accountability question. Share it with the group 24 hours before the session.

Peer Mentoring Techniques

GROW model · Weekly check-ins · Celebrating wins · Welfare concerns

Mentoring is about listening first. The temptation as an ambassador is to immediately offer advice — resist it. Understand the student's situation fully before suggesting any solutions.

The GROW Model

Use the GROW model as your framework for all mentoring conversations:

G

Goal

"What do you want to achieve in your studies this semester?" — Define a clear, measurable goal with the student.

R

Reality

"What's happening right now that's getting in the way?" — Understand the current situation without judgment.

O

Options

"What could you do differently?" — Brainstorm at least 3 options together before settling on one.

W

Way Forward

"What will you commit to doing before we speak next week?" — Agree on a concrete action and a follow-up date.

Weekly Check-Ins

A 5-minute WhatsApp check-in once per week is more effective than a monthly 30-minute meeting. Ask three questions: (1) How are your studies going this week? (2) What's one thing you've done well? (3) What do you need help with?

Escalating Welfare Concerns

If a student shares something serious — financial hardship, mental health struggles, family crisis — do not try to handle it alone. Your role is to listen and refer. Contact your campus coordinator immediately and guide the student to the appropriate university support service.

Role-Play Exercise

With a fellow ambassador, practise a GROW-model mentoring conversation. One person plays a student struggling with exam prep, the other plays the ambassador mentor. Switch roles after 10 minutes. Debrief: what questions worked best? What felt awkward?

Conflict Resolution

DESC framework · Early intervention · Shared goals · De-escalation

Conflict is inevitable in any team or group. As an ambassador, you will encounter disagreements — between students in a study group, between fellow ambassadors, or between a student and university processes. How you handle it defines your leadership credibility.

The DESC Framework

D

Describe the Behaviour

State what you observed factually, without judgment: "I noticed you arrived 20 minutes late to the last two sessions…"

E

Express How it Affects You

"…and it means the group has to restart or repeat content, which eats into our prep time."

S

Specify What You Need

"I'd appreciate it if you could message the group when you're running late so we can plan around it."

C

Commit to a Shared Outcome

"Can we agree on that? I want us all to get the most out of these sessions together."

Intervene Early

Small tensions addressed early stay small. Unresolved, they grow into group dysfunction. A 2-minute private conversation is infinitely easier than mediating a group fallout. Make it your habit to address issues within 24 hours of noticing them.

Scenario Exercise

Write out how you would use the DESC framework for the following scenario: Two students in your study group have been arguing about which topics to prioritise in sessions. Tensions are rising and one student has suggested leaving the group. How do you handle it?
