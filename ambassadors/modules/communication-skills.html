Foundations of Effective Communication

7-38-55 rule · Body language · Tailoring your message

Communication is far more than the words you say. Research consistently shows that 7% of a message's impact comes from words, 38% from tone/voice, and 55% from body language. As an ambassador presenting AZ Learner to strangers, your non-verbal presence often determines whether they trust you — before you've said a word.

Body Language Essentials

Open Posture

Face the person directly, uncrossed arms, slight lean forward. Signals engagement and confidence.

Eye Contact

Maintain natural eye contact (not staring). In a group, distribute your gaze to include everyone equally.

Speaking Pace

Slow down when making key points. Rushing signals nervousness. Pausing after important statements adds impact.

Gestures

Use natural hand gestures to emphasise points — but avoid excessive movement that distracts from your message.

Tailoring Your Message to the Audience

The same message delivered differently lands differently. When speaking to a lecturer's aide, be formal and data-driven. When speaking to freshers, be casual and relatable. When speaking at a conference, be polished and authoritative. Always ask: "Who is this person and what language do they speak?"

The Power of Silence

Most people are uncomfortable with silence and rush to fill it. Learn to pause deliberately after asking a question. Give people time to think. A 3-second silence feels much longer to the speaker than the listener — but it encourages deeper, more considered responses.

Observation Exercise

In your next social interaction with a student you're trying to recruit or mentor, consciously observe your own body language for the first 2 minutes. Are your arms crossed? Are you making eye contact? Are you speaking too quickly? Adjust deliberately and note the difference in how the conversation flows.

Public Speaking Fundamentals

3-T method · Managing nerves · 2-minute pitch · Delivery techniques

Public speaking is a learnable skill. The ambassadors who seem naturally confident on stage practised extensively in private. You will have to speak publicly — at events, in study groups, at campus fairs. This module gives you a reliable framework for every situation.

The 3-T Structure

T1

Tell them what you'll tell them

Open with a clear preview: "Today I'm going to show you three things AZ Learner can do for your studies."

T2

Tell them

Deliver your content clearly with supporting examples. One idea per section — never overload with information.

T3

Tell them what you told them

Close with a crisp summary and a strong call-to-action: "So — past questions, GPA tracking, study groups. Sign up free via the link I'm about to share."

Managing Nerves

  • • Deep breathing: 4 counts in, hold 4, out 4. Do this 3 times before stepping up to speak.
  • • Power posing: Stand in a confident posture (hands on hips, wide stance) for 2 minutes before speaking — research shows it measurably reduces cortisol.
  • • Focus outward: Shift attention from how you feel to how you can serve the audience. Nerves shrink when your focus is on being useful.

The 2-Minute AZ Learner Pitch (Memorise This)

"Hi everyone — I'm [Name], your AZ Learner campus ambassador. I want to tell you about a free platform that's been changing how students here study.

AZ Learner is built specifically for students in Ghana. It gives you instant access to past exam questions, a GPA calculator that actually updates in real-time, and study circles where you can find classmates in your courses to study with.

It's completely free to download and sign up. It takes under 2 minutes. I'll drop my referral link in the chat right now — every person who signs up via that link helps support the ambassador programme here on campus.

Any questions? Happy to show you on my phone."

Practice Exercise

Record yourself delivering the 2-minute pitch on your phone. Watch it back and evaluate: Did you use filler words? Was your pace too fast? Did you make eye contact with the camera? Re-record until you're satisfied. Submit the final version to your campus coordinator.

Digital & Written Communication

WhatsApp etiquette · Email structure · AZ Learner tone · Proofreading

As an ambassador, most of your student interactions happen in writing — WhatsApp messages, social media captions, emails to your campus coordinator. Poor written communication damages trust and credibility faster than almost anything else.

WhatsApp & Messaging Rules

One Topic Per Message

Don't put three separate asks in one message. Break them up and send separately if needed.

Clear Ask + Deadline

Every action-required message needs: what you want AND by when. "Please confirm by Friday 5PM" beats "let me know".

Bullet Points for Updates

When sharing multiple pieces of information, use bullet points. Wall-of-text messages get ignored or skimmed.

Proofread Before Sending

Read every message once before hitting send. Typos in ambassador communications create a poor impression of the platform.

The AZ Learner Tone

Every message you send as an ambassador represents the platform. The AZ Learner brand voice is: friendly, encouraging, clear, and honest. See the Communication Style Guide for full details and ready-made email/WhatsApp scripts.

Rewrite Exercise

Rewrite the following weak message in the AZ Learner brand voice:

"Hey guys so basically there's this study thing happening tomorrow at the library come if you can bring a friend also download the app"

Active Listening

Paraphrasing · Open questions · Summarising · Applying in study groups

Active listening means fully concentrating on what is being said rather than preparing your response while the other person speaks. It is the rarest and most powerful communication skill. Ambassadors who master it build deeper trust with students in half the time.

The 4 Active Listening Techniques

①

Paraphrase

Repeat back what you heard in your own words: "So it sounds like you're struggling with time management more than content understanding — is that right?"

②

Ask Open-Ended Questions

Questions that can't be answered with yes/no: "Can you tell me more about what's making revision feel so overwhelming right now?"

③

Avoid Interrupting

Let the student complete their full thought before responding. Count to 2 silently before you speak — this prevents cutting them off.

④

Summarise at the End

"Let me make sure I've understood: you need help with the study group structure and you'd benefit most from the past questions feature — right?" This confirms understanding and shows respect.

Applying in Study Groups

Use active listening to surface what students actually need from the session. Before starting, ask: "What's the one thing you want to understand better by the end of today?" Then design the session around those answers. This dramatically increases engagement and satisfaction.

5-Minute Exercise

In your next conversation with a student (about anything), practise active listening for the entire conversation. After it ends, write down: (1) What did you learn about them? (2) Did you interrupt at any point? (3) What open-ended question worked best?
